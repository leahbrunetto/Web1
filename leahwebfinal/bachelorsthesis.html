Bachelor's Thesis: Infinite Urban Landscapes

Fall 2011 - Spring 2012

Designed landscapes—which include public parks, engineered ponds and rivers, planted groups of trees, among others—are the only manifestations of the natural world that we have. In the same way that a painting is only a representation of a scene, these urban landscapes might be considered only symbols of true wilderness (Foster 141). Many city dwellers rarely travel to “untouched” nature. However, my argument is not that cities are limiting, but rather, that the juxtaposition of their architecture and infrastructure with landscape creates unique conditions for the millions of people who grow up in cities.Urban landscapes become infinite in terms of the combination of real experiences and dreams in landscape, and illusions of spaces as vaster than the really are. In this study, I investigate how these designed landscapes and their conditions contribute to developmental journeys in the city.

Approaching this concern as a visual artist, I begin this exploration in my hometown of Cambridge, Massachusetts. I examine illusions of landscape continuity and expansion, the history of constructed landscapes, the shared usage of green spaces, and ways in which such sites and their context are represented subjectively by artists. The sites which have provided me lifelong inspiration mark starting points for my research into how urban dwellers perceive, experience, and represent designed landscapes. I hope to provide legitimacy to those who have grown up or are growing up in cities, who do not have a big backyard, but found it elsewhere.
