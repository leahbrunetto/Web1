Second Home

Spring 2010

“I will curl up within the negative space of the sculpture (without touching it), surrounded by two of its triangular appendages; this is an analogy to a bear entering a cave for winter hibernation. I will lay a visible line of tape on the ground (also not touching the sculpture) connecting these two arms of the sculpture—and thus creating their hypotenuse—as a symbol of unifying myself, the performer, and the cavity of refuge. Afterward, I will reverse the process, leaving the garden empty.”

At MIT, students often lack personal spaces to return to during the course of a day, when they may be on campus for over twelve consecutive hours. Going from lecture to lunch, from lunch to lab, and so on, ideas, events, and spaces begin to blur. This project addresses these concerns through a performative intervention, in which I transformed the garden into a refuge by embodying the phenomenon of hibernation and the act of coming home. The site public, yet distant, space on campus: the Class of 2002 “Peace Garden”. While it is visible from corridors and accessible from the very active Stata Center, it is uninhabited. Coincidentally, students are very often seen sleeping on the in front of the nearby lecture hall. The audience, split vertically between the multiple corridors,viewed the performance through the glass side facing the garden. The garden served as stage, where each corridor level functions as a separate balcony and the performance site was viewed in architectural plan.
